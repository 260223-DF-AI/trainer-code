# Artificial Intelligence Fundamentals

## Learning Objectives

- Distinguish between Narrow AI and General AI
- Identify the core subfields of AI
- Understand key AI terminology used in professional settings
- Recognize the current capabilities and limitations of AI systems

## Why This Matters

AI terminology appears everywhere in job postings, product descriptions, and technical discussions. As data professionals, having a precise vocabulary for AI concepts allows you to communicate effectively with data scientists, ML engineers, and product teams. It also helps you critically evaluate AI claims and understand what these systems can and cannot do.

## The Concept

### Narrow AI vs General AI

#### Narrow AI (Weak AI)

Narrow AI is designed to perform a specific task. Every AI system you interact with today is Narrow AI:

- A spam filter classifying emails
- A recommendation engine suggesting videos
- A chatbot answering customer questions
- GitHub Copilot suggesting code completions

Narrow AI excels within its defined domain but cannot transfer its skills to other domains. A chess-playing AI cannot write poetry.

#### Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)

AGI refers to a hypothetical AI system with human-level reasoning across any domain. AGI does not exist today. It remains an active area of research and debate. When people discuss whether current LLMs are "approaching AGI," they are engaging in speculation, not describing current capabilities.

#### Artificial Superintelligence (ASI)

ASI refers to AI that surpasses human intelligence in every domain. This is a theoretical concept discussed in futurism and philosophy, not in current engineering practice.

### Core Subfields of AI

| Subfield | Focus | Example Application |
| -------- | ----- | ------------------- |
| Natural Language Processing (NLP) | Understanding and generating human language | Chatbots, translation, summarization |
| Computer Vision | Interpreting images and video | Object detection, facial recognition, medical imaging |
| Robotics | Physical systems that interact with the real world | Manufacturing robots, autonomous vehicles |
| Expert Systems | Rule-based decision making | Medical diagnosis support, financial compliance |
| Speech Recognition | Converting spoken language to text | Virtual assistants, transcription services |
| Generative AI | Creating new content from learned patterns | Text generation, image creation, code completion |

### Key Terminology

**Model**: A mathematical representation trained on data. Think of it as the "brain" that contains learned patterns.

**Training**: The process of feeding data to a model so it can learn patterns. Similar to how studying examples helps you learn a concept.

**Inference**: Using a trained model to make predictions on new data. This is the model "doing its job" after training is complete.

**Parameters**: The internal values a model adjusts during training. Larger models have more parameters (GPT-4 is estimated to have over a trillion parameters).

**Neural Network**: A computing architecture inspired by the human brain, consisting of layers of interconnected nodes that process information.

**Transformer**: The specific neural network architecture behind modern LLMs. Introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need," transformers revolutionized NLP.

### Current Capabilities and Limitations

**What AI Can Do Well:**

- Process and analyze massive datasets
- Identify patterns humans might miss
- Generate human-like text, images, and code
- Automate repetitive cognitive tasks
- Operate at scale and speed

**What AI Cannot Do:**

- Truly understand meaning or context the way humans do
- Guarantee factual accuracy (hallucinations are a persistent challenge)
- Exercise genuine creativity or consciousness
- Apply common sense reasoning reliably
- Replace human judgment in critical decisions

## Key Takeaways

- All current AI systems are Narrow AI, designed for specific tasks
- AI is a broad field with many specialized subfields
- Understanding AI terminology helps you communicate with cross-functional teams
- AI systems have impressive capabilities but also significant limitations that must be accounted for
